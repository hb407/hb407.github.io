About Me

I am the Astor Junior Research Fellow in Chemistry at New College, Oxford and a postdoctoral researcher in the Department of Chemistry, University of Oxford. My primary research involves understanding the breakdown of electronic structure approximations in the presence of strongly coupled electrons and developing new theoretical methods to accurately predict the properties of electrons in molecules. In particular, I am interested in the failure of independent particle models in the presence of strong correlation, and the existence of multiple solutions to the self-consistent field equations. My other projects include exploiting nonorthogonal many-electron basis states for describing strong correlation in molecules, and constructing non-Hermitian analytic continuations that allow an electronic ground state to be smoothly morphed into an excited state.

I was previously a postdoctoral research associate with Prof. David Wales at the University of Cambridge, U.K. During this time, I developed the electronic structure energy landscape framework where approximate electronic states exist as stationary points of the corresponding electronic energy surface. This approach allows the properties and connectivity of multiple electronic stationary points to be systematically investigated, providing guiding principles for the development of state-specific excited-state methods.

I completed my PhD under the supervision of Dr Alex Thom at the University of Cambridge in 2020. During this period, I pioneered the holomorphic Hartree-Fock approach for generating continuous Hartree-Fock solutions as a basis for multireference calculations. In holomorphic Hartree-Fock theory, conventional Hartree-Fock solutions are analytically continued into the complex plane beyond the molecular structures at which they vanish. My thesis showed that construcing a linear combination of these solutions allows qualitatively accurate electronic energies to be predicted for bond-breaking processes and molecular binding curves.

In the summer of 2018, I undertook a two-month research internship at Q-Chem Inc., an electronic structure software package based in Pleasanton CA, USA. At Q-Chem, I developed a new dedicated library for running nonorthogonal configuration interaction calcuations on molecules. This library is now available in latest public release of the Q-Chem software package.

Prior to my PhD, I studied chemistry at the University of Cambridge. As part of my studies, I completed a research project with Prof. Daan Frenkel, investigating the thermophoretic flow of atoms under the influence of temperature graidents at liquid-liquid interfaces.
